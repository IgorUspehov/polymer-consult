# Recycled Polyethylene in the European Union: Comprehensive Market Analysis of LDPE and HDPE

## 1. Introduction: why recycled polyethylene has become a strategic raw material

The European Union has entered the era of the circular economy, where waste ceases to be rubbish and turns into a valuable resource. Low-density polyethylene (LDPE) and high-density polyethylene (HDPE) are at the centre of this transformation. Together, these two materials account for more than 45% of the entire EU plastics market, and their recycling has been growing at 7–9% per year since 2020.

What drives this growth? First, legislation. The European Commission has adopted a package of measures that will make the use of at least 30% recycled plastic in packaging mandatory by 2030. Second, economics. The spread between virgin and recycled polyethylene reaches €200–350 per tonne, making recyclate profitable for manufacturers. Third, environmental requirements of major brands: Unilever, Procter & Gamble, Henkel and Nestlé have publicly announced a transition to packaging containing 50–100% recycled polymers by 2025.

This article provides a comprehensive overview of the recycled LDPE and HDPE market in the EU, with a focus on Germany as the recycling leader. We examine feedstock sources, key recyclers, production volumes, prices, legislation and examples of end products made from recyclates.

## 2. Feedstock: where LDPE and HDPE waste comes from

### 2.1. LDPE streams
Low-density polyethylene comes mainly from packaging materials. Main sources:
- **Stretch film** (logistics, warehouse complexes). Clean industrial waste yields up to 95% high-quality regranulate.
- **Carrier bags and T-shirt bags**. After sorting at waste processing plants, fractions containing 60–70% LDPE are extracted from "yellow bags".
- **Agricultural film** (silage, greenhouse, mulching). In Germany, France and the Netherlands, take-back systems for agricultural film operate. Contamination with soil and organic matter reduces yield to 50–70%, but modern washing lines can handle this.
- **Industrial sacks and liners** (for construction mixtures, chemicals, food). This is a high-quality stream with minimal inclusions.

### 2.2. HDPE streams
High-density polyethylene is more rigid and chemically resistant. Main sources:
- **Detergent and household chemical bottles** (shampoos, gels, bleaches). This is the cleanest post-consumer stream: white and natural bottles provide up to 90% usable material.
- **Canisters and drums** (industrial, from oils, solvents, food additives). Thorough washing from residues is necessary.
- **Pipes and fittings** (construction sector, municipal utilities). Often coloured black or orange, which limits application to technical products only.
- **Caps and closures** from beverages. Since 2021, under the SUP Directive, they must remain attached to bottles, which has simplified their collection and sorting.

### 2.3. Role of the separate collection system in Germany
Germany is a benchmark for collection. The "yellow bag" (Gelber Sack) and "yellow bin" (Gelbe Tonne) system covers 100% of households. Around 3.2 million tonnes of PE‑containing waste are collected annually. Sorting lines use NIR scanners (near infrared), which separate LDPE from HDPE, and then from PET, PP, PS and PVC with 98% accuracy.

The share of post-consumer feedstock in total PE recycling volume in Germany reached 58% in 2023 (up from 42% in 2018). Industrial waste accounts for the remaining 42%, but its volume is decreasing due to production optimisation.

## 3. Largest recyclers of polyethylene in the EU

### 3.1. Germany – the absolute leader
Germany accounts for 31% of all PE recycling capacity in Europe.

**ALBA Group (Berlin)** – one of the oldest players. Total capacity for LDPE and HDPE exceeds 120,000 tonnes per year. ALBA focuses on closed-loop systems for retail chains: it collects stretch film from distribution centres, recycles it and returns it as new film.

**REMONDIS Recycling (Lünen)** – specialises in HDPE. Capacity 85,000 tonnes per year, of which 40,000 tonnes is food‑grade regranulate certified by EFSA (European Food Safety Authority). REMONDIS was the first in Germany to introduce deodorisation technology to remove detergent odours from bottles.

**PreZero (Neustadt)** – a subsidiary of the Schwarz Group (Lidl, Kaufland). Processes post-consumer blends and issues a RecyClass certificate recognised by all major brands. Capacity – 70,000 tonnes of PE per year.

**mtm plastics (Niedergebra, Thuringia)** – Europe's largest plant for mixed LDPE films. Capacity 75,000 tonnes per year. Unique feature: it can recycle film with printing, labels and small inclusions of other polymers without quality loss.

**Interseroh (Cologne)** – part of the ALBA Group but operates as a separate brand. Capacity 50,000 tonnes of LDPE/year. Focus on industrial packaging.

### 3.2. France
**Paprec** – the largest private recycler in France. Processes 60,000 tonnes of HDPE and 40,000 tonnes of LDPE per year. Plants in Paris, Lyon and Bordeaux.

**Veolia** – global water and waste giant. In France, PE capacity is 55,000 tonnes/year. Veolia has developed the "PlastiLoop" technology – selling recyclate with guaranteed properties.

**Suez** (now part of Veolia) – an additional 30,000 tonnes of PE.

### 3.3. Italy
**Aliplast** – a division of the Hera Group. Processes 50,000 tonnes of LDPE per year, mainly agricultural film and stretch. Aliplast produces black and grey regranulate for construction films.

**Montello** – one of Italy's oldest recyclers (founded in 1988). Capacity 45,000 tonnes of HDPE, specialising in detergent bottles.

**Sorema** – an engineering company that also recycles. Capacity 30,000 tonnes of LDPE/year.

### 3.4. Other countries
- **Netherlands**: Umincorp (magnetic separation, 25,000 tonnes of mixed PE).
- **Spain**: Acteco (30,000 tonnes of HDPE).
- **Poland**: Anwen (20,000 tonnes of LDPE), one of the fastest-growing markets due to cheap labour.

In total, the EU has about 380 mechanical PE recycling plants, of which 120 are in Germany.

## 4. Recycled PE production volumes (2023)

According to Plastics Europe and Conversio (Market Study on Plastic Recycling), data for 2023:

| Material | Volume (million tonnes) | Germany's share |
|----------|------------------------|----------------|
| LDPE regranulate | 1.9 | 620 kt (33%) |
| HDPE regranulate | 2.5 | 810 kt (32%) |
| **Total PE** | **4.4** | **1.43 Mt (32.5%)** |

Forecast for 2026: LDPE – 2.6 Mt, HDPE – 3.4 Mt, total 6.0 Mt. Growth rate 10–12% per year due to new capacities and increased recyclate targets.

Not all collected PE waste is recycled. In 2023, about 6.8 million tonnes of PE waste were collected in the EU. Of this, 4.4 million tonnes were recycled into regranulate (65%), 2.0 million tonnes went to energy recovery (incineration), and 0.4 million tonnes were landfilled (mainly in Eastern Europe, but the share is decreasing).

## 5. Prices for recycled polyethylene in Germany (2024)

Pricing for recyclate depends on three factors: colour/purity, application (food or technical), and form (flakes, agglomerate, regranulate). Below are average ex‑works prices as of April 2024.

### LDPE
- **Clear food‑grade regranulate** (MFI 0.3–3, clean, odourless): €890–1020/t. Demand exceeds supply by 1.5 times.
- **Semi‑clear/white regranulate** (for non‑food contact films): €760–850/t.
- **Dark (grey, black) regranulate** (technical, construction films, waste bags): €620–740/t. The most affordable and least scarce.
- **LDPE agglomerate** (non‑granulated, dense lumps): €500–600/t. Bought by producers who have their own granulation.

### HDPE
- **Natural (milky, beige) food‑grade regranulate**: €980–1150/t. The most expensive due to shortage of clean bottles.
- **Blue or green regranulate** (for household chemical bottles): €850–950/t.
- **Black/dark grey technical regranulate** (pipes, pallets, crates): €700–820/t.

### For comparison: virgin PE (spot prices, CIF North‑West Europe)
- Virgin LDPE: €1150–1300/t
- Virgin HDPE (injection): €1100–1200/t

The spread of €200–300/t makes recycled PE attractive for large manufacturers, especially considering environmental taxes.

Trend for 2024: recyclate prices rose 5–7% due to the energy crisis (expensive electricity for washing and granulation) and shortage of quality waste. Reverse trend: virgin PE prices stabilised and even fell slightly due to falling demand in China's construction sector.

## 6. EU laws and directives regulating the use of recycled polymers

### 6.1. EU Plastics Strategy (2018)
Framework document that set the goal: by 2030, all plastic packaging on the EU market must be reusable or economically recyclable. For PE, this meant banning multilayer structures that cannot be separated (e.g. PE + aluminium).

### 6.2. Directive on Single-Use Plastics (SUP, 2019/904)
Entered into force on 3 July 2021. Ten types of single‑use plastics are banned, including cotton buds, straws, stirrers, plates, cutlery made of PE foam. Recycled content targets for PET bottles (25% by 2025, 30% by 2030). For PE bottles (milk, juices, sauces) – recommended targets of 15–20%, but national laws make them mandatory (see below).

### 6.3. German Packaging Act (VerpackG) – 2019/2021
One of the strictest national laws. Key provisions:
- Packaging producers must register in the LUCID register.
- Licensing fees depend on the share of recyclate. Packaging without recyclate is subject to a 20–50% surcharge.
- From 2023: reporting on actual recyclate use (audited).
- From 2025: plan to achieve 50% recyclate in PE packaging for dairy products (retail requirement effectively became mandatory).

### 6.4. PPWR (Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation) – adopted in 2024
Replaces the old Directive 94/62/EC. This is a directly applicable regulation:
- By 1 January 2030: at least 30% recyclate in any plastic packaging (including PE).
- By 1 January 2040: at least 65% for single‑use plastic packaging.
- Ban on PFAS (forever chemicals) in food‑contact packaging – which simplifies PE recycling.

### 6.5. France: AGEC Law (Anti‑Waste, 2021)
- Ban on destruction of unsold non‑food products.
- From 2025: 30% recyclate in all plastic packaging.
- Bonus‑malus on plastics: manufacturer pays less eco‑fee if using recyclate.

### 6.6. Italy: virgin plastic tax
From 2024 (delayed several times) – €0.45 per kilogram of plastic packaging made from virgin raw material. Tax not applied if packaging contains at least 30% recyclate. Effectively makes recycled PE 15–20% cheaper.

### 6.7. Spain: tax on single‑use plastic
€0.45/kg on all single‑use plastic packaging manufactured or imported into Spain. Exception – if packaging contains at least 50% recyclate and this is certified.

## 7. End products with recycled polymers

Recyclate from LDPE and HDPE is used in dozens of products. The largest segments with real examples:

### 7.1. Waste bags and recycling bags
Up to 100% recycled LDPE. Major manufacturers: ALDI (own bags), RPC bpi Group, Berry Global. Retail price 30–40% lower than virgin PE.

### 7.2. Sewage and drainage pipes
HDPE recyclate (black, technical) – up to 60% content. Manufacturers: Pipelife, Wavin, REHAU. DIN CERTCO and KIWA certification guarantee 50+ years durability.

### 7.3. Laminate underlays and construction films
LDPE recyclate (mixed, often grey) – 30–70%. Knauf uses 80% recyclate in its Knauf PE underlay. Vapour barrier films on construction sites – up to 50% recyclate.

### 7.4. Crates, pallets, logistics containers
HDPE recyclate (injection grades) – 50–80%. Example: logistics company CHEP produces returnable pallets from 100% recyclate.

### 7.5. Detergent and household chemical bottles
HDPE with 50–100% PCR. Brands: Henkel (Persil, Bref), Procter & Gamble (Fairy, Ariel), Unilever (Domestos). By 2025, all bottles in the EU must contain 50% PCR.

### 7.6. Garden furniture, traffic cones, street bins
Black/dark HDPE – up to 100% recyclate. IKEA (KUNGSBACKA series) – 70% recyclate. Traffic cones from recycled HDPE are 40% cheaper than virgin ones.

### 7.7. Automotive mats and load floors
Mixed HDPE recyclate – up to 50%. Treves produces for Renault and Volkswagen.

### 7.8. Stretch wrap for palletising
LDPE recyclate (clear or semi‑clear) – 30–50%. Used for secondary packaging (not for direct food contact).

## 8. Conclusion

The recycled polyethylene market in the European Union has moved from the experimental phase to industrial scaling. Germany maintains its lead thanks to a well‑developed collection system, processing capacities (380 plants in the EU, a third of them in Germany) and strict legislation that makes recyclate economically viable.

Key findings:
1. **Volumes** – 4.4 million tonnes of PE recyclate in 2023, growing to 6 million tonnes by 2026.
2. **Prices** – spread with virgin PE of €200–350/t ensures payback of recycling without subsidies.
3. **Laws** – PPWR, VerpackG, AGEC and virgin plastics taxes create mandatory demand for recyclate.
4. **Products** – from waste bags to food‑grade bottles, recyclate has already replaced virgin PE in thousands of products.

Shortage of high‑quality clean streams of LDPE and HDPE remains the main constraint. The solution is expanded sorting (NIR, AI recognition) and chemical recycling for complex contaminated fractions. Nevertheless, mechanical recycling will remain the dominant technology for PE for the next 5–7 years.

Packaging producers and plastics recyclers are advised to enter into long‑term contracts for recyclate supply, as prices will rise 5–10% annually and shortages will persist until at least 2028.
